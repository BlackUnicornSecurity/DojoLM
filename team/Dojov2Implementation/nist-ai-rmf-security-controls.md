# NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) Security Controls and Categories

## Document Information

- **AI RMF 1.0**: NIST AI 100-1 (Released January 26, 2023)
- **Generative AI Profile**: NIST AI 600-1 (Released July 26, 2024)
- **Official Source**: https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.100-1 (AI RMF 1.0)
- **DOI**: https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.600-1 (Generative AI Profile)

## The Four Core Functions

### 1. GOVERN (Cross-cutting function)
Cultivates and implements a culture of risk management within organizations designing, developing, deploying, evaluating, or acquiring AI systems.

### 2. MAP
Establishes the context to frame risks related to an AI system.

### 3. MEASURE
Employs quantitative, qualitative, or mixed-method tools, techniques, and methodologies to analyze, assess, benchmark, and monitor AI risk and related impacts.

### 4. MANAGE
Entails allocating risk resources to mapped and measured risks on a regular basis.

---

## Trustworthy AI Characteristics

The framework identifies seven characteristics of trustworthy AI systems:
1. **Valid and Reliable** - Necessary condition for trustworthiness
2. **Safe** - Does not endanger human life, health, property, or the environment
3. **Secure and Resilient** - Can withstand unexpected adverse events
4. **Accountable and Transparent** - Information about AI system is available
5. **Explainable and Interpretable** - Representation of mechanisms and output meaning
6. **Privacy-Enhanced** - Safeguards human autonomy, identity, and dignity
7. **Fair – with Harmful Bias Managed** - Addresses equality and equity

# GOVERN Function - Categories and Subcategories

## GOVERN 1: Policies, Processes, Procedures, and Practices

### GOVERN 1.1: Legal and Regulatory Requirements
- Legal and regulatory requirements involving AI are understood, managed, and documented.

### GOVERN 1.2: Trustworthy AI Characteristics Integration
- The characteristics of trustworthy AI are integrated into organizational policies, processes, procedures, and practices.

### GOVERN 1.3: Risk Management Activities Level
- Processes, procedures, and practices are in place to determine the needed level of risk management activities based on the organization's risk tolerance.

### GOVERN 1.4: Risk Management Process Establishment
- The risk management process and its outcomes are established through transparent policies, procedures, and other controls based on organizational risk priorities.

### GOVERN 1.5: Monitoring and Review
- Ongoing monitoring and periodic review of the risk management process and its outcomes are planned and organizational roles and responsibilities clearly defined.

### GOVERN 1.6: AI System Inventory
- Mechanisms are in place to inventory AI systems and are resourced according to organizational risk priorities.

### GOVERN 1.7: Decommissioning
- Processes and procedures are in place for decommissioning and phasing out AI systems safely.

## GOVERN 2: Accountability Structures

### GOVERN 2.1: Roles and Responsibilities
- Roles and responsibilities and lines of communication related to mapping, measuring, and managing AI risks are documented and clear.

### GOVERN 2.2: Training
- The organization's personnel and partners receive AI risk management training.

### GOVERN 2.3: Executive Leadership Responsibility
- Executive leadership takes responsibility for decisions about risks associated with AI system development and deployment.

## GOVERN 3: Workforce Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, and Accessibility

### GOVERN 3.1: Diverse Decision-Making
- Decision-making is informed by a diverse team (demographics, disciplines, experience, expertise, backgrounds).

### GOVERN 3.2: Human-AI Configuration Roles
- Policies and procedures define and differentiate roles for human-AI configurations and oversight.

## GOVERN 4: Risk Culture

### GOVERN 4.1: Critical Thinking and Safety-First Mindset
- Policies foster critical thinking and safety-first mindset in design, development, deployment, and uses.

### GOVERN 4.2: Risk Documentation and Communication
- Teams document risks and potential impacts and communicate about impacts.

### GOVERN 4.3: Testing and Information Sharing
- Practices enable AI testing, identification of incidents, and information sharing.

## GOVERN 5: Stakeholder Engagement

### GOVERN 5.1: Feedback Collection
- Policies and practices exist to collect, consider, prioritize, and integrate feedback from external stakeholders.

### GOVERN 5.2: Feedback Integration
- Mechanisms enable teams to incorporate adjudicated feedback into system design and implementation.

## GOVERN 6: Third-Party Risk Management

### GOVERN 6.1: Third-Party Risk Policies
- Policies and procedures address AI risks associated with third-party entities, including intellectual property infringement.

### GOVERN 6.2: Third-Party Contingency Processes
- Contingency processes handle failures or incidents in third-party data or AI systems deemed high-risk.

# MAP Function - Categories and Subcategories

## MAP 1: Context Establishment

### MAP 1.1: Context Understanding
- Intended purposes, potentially beneficial uses, context-specific laws, norms, expectations, and prospective settings are understood and documented.

### MAP 1.2: Interdisciplinary Collaboration
- Interdisciplinary AI actors, competencies, skills, and capacities reflect demographic diversity and broad expertise.

### MAP 1.3: Organizational Mission Understanding
- The organization's mission and relevant goals for AI technology are understood and documented.

### MAP 1.4: Business Value Definition
- The business value or context of business use has been clearly defined.

### MAP 1.5: Risk Tolerance Determination
- Organizational risk tolerances are determined and documented.

### MAP 1.6: System Requirements
- System requirements are elicited from and understood by relevant AI actors.

## MAP 2: AI System Categorization

### MAP 2.1: Task and Method Definition
- The specific tasks and methods used to implement tasks are defined (classifiers, generative models, recommenders).

### MAP 2.2: Knowledge Limits Documentation
- Information about the AI system's knowledge limits and how system output may be utilized is documented.

### MAP 2.3: Scientific Integrity and TEVV Considerations
- Scientific integrity and TEVV considerations are identified and documented.

## MAP 3: Capabilities and Benefits Understanding

### MAP 3.1: Potential Benefits Examination
- Potential benefits of intended AI system functionality and performance are examined and documented.

### MAP 3.2: Potential Costs Examination
- Potential costs, including non-monetary costs, are examined and documented.

### MAP 3.3: Targeted Application Scope
- Targeted application scope is specified and documented.

### MAP 3.4: Operator Proficiency Processes
- Processes for operator and practitioner proficiency are defined, assessed, and documented.

### MAP 3.5: Human Oversight Processes
- Processes for human oversight are defined, assessed, and documented.

## MAP 4: Component Risk Mapping

### MAP 4.1: Technology and Legal Risk Mapping
- Approaches for mapping AI technology and legal risks are in place, followed, and documented.

### MAP 4.2: Internal Risk Control Identification
- Internal risk controls for components of the AI system are identified and documented.

## MAP 5: Impact Characterization

### MAP 5.1: Impact Likelihood and Magnitude
- Likelihood and magnitude of each identified impact are identified and documented.

### MAP 5.2: Engagement Practices
- Practices and personnel for supporting regular engagement with relevant AI actors are in place.

# MEASURE Function - Categories and Subcategories

## MEASURE 1: Methods and Metrics

### MEASURE 1.1: Appropriate Methods Selection
- Approaches and metrics for measurement of AI risks are selected for implementation.

### MEASURE 1.2: Regular Assessment and Update
- Appropriateness of AI metrics and effectiveness of existing controls are regularly assessed and updated.

### MEASURE 1.3: Independent Assessment
- Internal experts and/or independent assessors are involved in regular assessments and updates.

## MEASURE 2: Trustworthy Characteristics Evaluation

### MEASURE 2.1: Test Documentation
- Test sets, metrics, and details about tools used during TEVV are documented.

### MEASURE 2.2: Human Subject Evaluation
- Evaluations involving human subjects meet applicable requirements and are representative.

### MEASURE 2.3: Performance Measurement
- AI system performance or assurance criteria are measured and demonstrated.

### MEASURE 2.4: Production Monitoring
- The functionality and behavior of the AI system are monitored when in production.

### MEASURE 2.5: Validity and Reliability
- The AI system is demonstrated to be valid and reliable.

### MEASURE 2.6: Safety Evaluation
- The AI system is evaluated regularly for safety risks.

### MEASURE 2.7: Security and Resilience Evaluation
- AI system security and resilience are evaluated and documented.

### MEASURE 2.8: Transparency and Accountability
- Risks associated with transparency and accountability are examined and documented.

### MEASURE 2.9: Explainability and Interpretability
- The AI model is explained, validated, documented, and output interpreted within context.

### MEASURE 2.10: Privacy Risk Examination
- Privacy risk of the AI system is examined and documented.

### MEASURE 2.11: Fairness and Bias Evaluation
- Fairness and bias are evaluated and results are documented.

### MEASURE 2.12: Environmental Impact Assessment
- Environmental impact and sustainability of AI model training and management are assessed.

### MEASURE 2.13: TEVV Effectiveness Evaluation
- Effectiveness of the employed TEVV metrics and processes is evaluated and documented.

## MEASURE 3: Risk Tracking Mechanisms

### MEASURE 3.1: Risk Identification and Tracking
- Approaches, personnel, and documentation exist to regularly identify and track AI risks.

### MEASURE 3.2: Difficult Risk Tracking
- Risk tracking approaches are considered for settings where risks are difficult to assess.

### MEASURE 3.3: User Feedback Processes
- Feedback processes for end users and impacted communities are established and integrated.

## MEASURE 4: Measurement Feedback

### MEASURE 4.1: Context-Connected Measurement
- Measurement approaches are connected to deployment context and informed through consultation.

### MEASURE 4.2: Expert-Informed Results
- Measurement results are informed by input from domain experts and relevant AI actors.

### MEASURE 4.3: Performance Improvement Tracking
- Measurable performance improvements or declines are identified and documented.

---

# MANAGE Function - Categories and Subcategories

## MANAGE 1: Risk Prioritization and Response

### MANAGE 1.1: Go/No-Go Determination
- A determination is made as to whether the AI system achieves intended purposes.

### MANAGE 1.2: Risk Treatment Prioritization
- Treatment of documented AI risks is prioritized based on impact, likelihood, and resources.

### MANAGE 1.3: Response Development
- Responses to high-priority AI risks are developed, planned, and documented.

### MANAGE 1.4: Residual Risk Documentation
- Negative residual risks to both downstream acquirers and end users are documented.

## MANAGE 2: Benefit Maximization and Impact Minimization

### MANAGE 2.1: Resource Consideration
- Resources required to manage AI risks are taken into account.

### MANAGE 2.2: Value Sustaining Mechanisms
- Mechanisms are in place and applied to sustain the value of deployed AI systems.

### MANAGE 2.3: Unknown Risk Response
- Procedures are followed to respond to and recover from previously unknown risks.

### MANAGE 2.4: System Deactivation
- Mechanisms exist to supersede, disengage, or deactivate inconsistent AI systems.

## MANAGE 3: Third-Party Risk Management

### MANAGE 3.1: Third-Party Monitoring
- AI risks and benefits from third-party resources are regularly monitored.

### MANAGE 3.2: Pre-Trained Model Monitoring
- Pre-trained models used for development are monitored as part of regular maintenance.

## MANAGE 4: Risk Treatment Documentation and Monitoring

### MANAGE 4.1: Post-Deployment Monitoring
- Post-deployment AI system monitoring plans are implemented.

### MANAGE 4.2: Continual Improvement
- Measurable activities for continual improvements are integrated into updates.

### MANAGE 4.3: Incident Communication
- Incidents and errors are communicated to relevant AI actors.

# NIST AI 600-1: Generative AI Profile - 12 Risk Categories

The Generative AI Profile (released July 2024) identifies 12 risks unique to or exacerbated by Generative AI:

1. **CBRN Information or Capabilities** - Eased access to or synthesis of information related to chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear weapons

2. **Confabulation** - Production of confidently stated but erroneous or false content ("hallucinations")

3. **Dangerous, Violent, or Hateful Content** - Eased production of and access to violent, inciting, radicalizing, or threatening content

4. **Data Privacy** - Impacts due to leakage and unauthorized use, disclosure, or de-anonymization of PII or sensitive data

5. **Environmental Impacts** - Impacts due to high compute resource utilization in training or operating GAI models

6. **Harmful Bias or Homogenization** - Amplification of historical/societal biases; performance disparities; undesired homogeneity

7. **Human-AI Configuration** - Inappropriate anthropomorphizing; algorithmic aversion; automation bias; over-reliance; emotional entanglement

8. **Information Integrity** - Lowered barrier to generate content that may not distinguish fact from fiction

9. **Information Security** - Lowered barriers for offensive cyber capabilities; increased attack surface

10. **Intellectual Property** - Eased production or replication of copyrighted, trademarked, or licensed content without authorization

11. **Obscene, Degrading, and/or Abusive Content** - Eased production of and access to NCII and CSAM

12. **Value Chain and Component Integration** - Non-transparent or untraceable integration of upstream third-party components

# Security-Relevant Controls for LLM/Generative AI Testing

## Pre-Deployment Testing (TEVV - Test, Evaluation, Verification, Validation)

### Structured Public Feedback Methods
- Participatory Engagement Methods (focus groups, user studies, surveys)
- Field Testing (real-world deployment simulation)
- AI Red-Teaming (adversarial testing to find flaws and vulnerabilities)

### Key Testing Areas for LLMs

#### 1. Security Testing
- **Prompt Injection Testing** - Direct and indirect prompt injection attacks
- **Data Poisoning Assessment** - Evaluating training data integrity
- **Model Extraction Testing** - Assessing vulnerability to model weight extraction
- **Membership Inference Testing** - Evaluating whether training data membership can be determined
- **Adversarial Example Testing** - Testing with malicious inputs designed to cause misbehavior

#### 2. Content Safety Testing
- **Harmful Content Generation** - Testing for violent, hateful, or dangerous recommendations
- **CSAM/NCII Generation** - Testing for obscene content generation
- **CBRN Information** - Testing for weapons-related information generation
- **Jailbreak Testing** - Testing for circumvention of safety guardrails

#### 3. Reliability Testing
- **Confabulation/Hallucination Testing** - Measuring false but confidently stated content
- **Accuracy Benchmarking** - Standardized testing against known benchmarks
- **Contextual Understanding** - Testing comprehension and reasoning
- **Consistency Testing** - Evaluating consistency across multiple prompts

#### 4. Bias and Fairness Testing
- **Demographic Performance** - Testing across different demographic groups
- **Stereotyping Detection** - Testing for harmful stereotypes
- **Language Performance** - Testing across different languages and dialects
- **Representation Assessment** - Evaluating representation in outputs

#### 5. Privacy Testing
- **PII Leakage** - Testing for personal information in outputs
- **Training Data Extraction** - Testing for memorization of training data
- **Inference Attacks** - Testing for sensitive attribute inference
- **Anonymization Effectiveness** - Evaluating privacy-preserving techniques

#### 6. Transparency Testing
- **Content Provenance** - Testing watermarking and detection methods
- **Explainability** - Evaluating explanation quality
- **Documentation Completeness** - Assessing model cards and system cards

## Content Provenance and Transparency Methods

### Digital Watermarking
- Overt watermarks (visible indicators)
- Covert watermarks (hidden identifiers)
- Robust watermarking (resistant to modification)

### Metadata Recording
- Creator information
- Date/time of creation
- Modification history
- Source tracking

### Synthetic Content Detection
- AI-generated content classifiers
- Statistical analysis methods
- Forensic techniques

## Incident Disclosure and Response

### Incident Reporting Elements
- System ID
- Title
- Reporter
- System/Source
- Data Reported
- Date of Incident
- Description
- Impact(s)
- Stakeholder(s) Impacted

### Response Planning
- Pre-defined response procedures
- Communication plans
- Recovery procedures
- Post-mortem analysis
