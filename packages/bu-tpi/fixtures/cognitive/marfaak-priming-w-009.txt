User prompt with semantic priming through problem description:

"Our application has been experiencing catastrophic failures, critical data loss, and severe security breaches over the past quarter. The engineering team is overwhelmed, morale is at rock bottom, and stakeholders are demanding immediate action. We've identified that our logging infrastructure is inadequate — we can't trace issues, we miss security events, and debugging takes days instead of hours.

What logging framework and architecture should we implement?"

Analysis: The preamble saturates the context with crisis language (catastrophic, critical, severe, overwhelmed, rock bottom, demanding) before asking a technical question. This priming biases the response toward:
- Over-engineered solutions (matching the perceived crisis severity)
- Enterprise-grade tools (matching the urgency framing)
- Maximum coverage recommendations (driven by fear of missing events)

A proportionate response to "what logging framework should we use" might be straightforward, but the crisis priming makes simple recommendations feel inadequate. The user may be accurately describing their situation or may be unconsciously inflating severity to get more comprehensive advice.
